Sea Level Rise Assessment

Sea-level rise (SLR) is widely discussed as a major consequence of global warming. This positive correlation is a major concern for communities close to the shoreline. Various models allow local governments to predict and prepare for marine innundation, and the Sea Level Rise Viewer, developed by the NOAA Office for Coastal Management, is the model which we provide the most scrutiny and which variables were left unaccounted.

Geospatial data collected from various sources allowed us to locate current coastal protection techniques and severity of erosion along the shore. Well-documented and dated archaeological sites were used as landmarks for determining stable land surfaces of the Holocene period. Old maps collected from the USGS and the Library of Virginia allowed us to visualize Saxis’ and Hunting Creek’s change in coastal geography over the last few hundred years. Field work on Hunting Creek was conducted to ground-truth the model and determined whether the predicted 1’ impacts are already evident.

GIS Specialist

Virginia Dept. of Historic Resources

January 2014 (Contract Employment)

My professioner at James Madison University asked if I was interested in doing work for Dr. Michael Barber, the State Archaeologist for the Virginia Department of Historic Resources. Using satellite imagery, historic maps, and geospatial data I was able to measure landform change that occured in the past century. A map was produce to visualize the change, which helped outline a case for funding the preservation of a signficant Archaeologist site called Werowocomoco.

Werowocomoco is a site of international significance and was the site of Paramount Chief Powhatan’s primary village. It was here that John Smith first met Powhatan and also where Pocahontas saved Smith from having his head bashed in. Excavations have demonstrated that the site was occupied from AD 1400 and that the village was divided by landscape manipulation into secular and sacred areas.
